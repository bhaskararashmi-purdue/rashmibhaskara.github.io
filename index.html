A tumor is not an island. It exists within a dynamic microenvironment of immune cells, hypoxic regions, and stromal tissue that continuously evolves as the disease progresses, and it is precisely how this microenvironment changes during treatment that determines whether a patient responds or resists. Capturing this biology today requires either a biopsy or a battery of specialized PET tracers, each targeting a different component: FAPI for fibroblast activity, FMISO for hypoxia, and so on. For a patient already navigating a difficult treatment journey, this means more injections, more procedures, and more distress. My research takes a different approach. Studies show that standard FDG PET carries meaningful signal about immune activity, hypoxia, and cancer-associated fibroblast behavior. I build deep learning models that extract these biological signatures from FDG PET and CT scans alone. Tracking how they evolve across treatment gives clinicians a window into the tumor microenvironment without any additional burden on the patient.

Ph.D. Dissertation In progress

Non-Invasive Tumor Microenvironment Characterization and Treatment Response Modeling in NSCLC

Advisor: Dr. Oluwaseyi Oderinde · AdMIRe Lab · Expected 2028

Aim 1
CT to FDG-PET synthesis

Training a 3D conditional optimal transport flow matching model on paired CT/PET from NSCLC-Radiogenomics (n=211) to generate metabolic maps for CT-only patients.

Aim 2
Biological TME state inference

Cross-modal VAE with ViT encoder supervised by RNA-seq-derived immune, hypoxic, and stromal scores to learn a biologically interpretable latent TME state per patient from PET/CT alone.

Aim 3
TME trajectory and survival prediction

Computing per-patient TME trajectory vectors before and after chemoRT and demonstrating improved survival concordance over RECIST and delta-SUVmax baselines.

What is the tumor microenvironment?

The TME is an ecosystem of cell types surrounding the tumor. Each plays a role in how the tumor grows and how it responds to treatment.

The problem with current TME measurement

Characterizing each TME component today requires separate specialized tracers or an invasive biopsy. My research extracts all of this from scans the patient already receives.

Tracking how the TME evolves during treatment

By applying the model at baseline and after chemoradiotherapy, we compute a biological trajectory per patient and ask whether immune activation or stromal remodeling predicts survival.
